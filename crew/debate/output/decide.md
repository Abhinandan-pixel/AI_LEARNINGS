After carefully considering the arguments from both sides, the position opposing a legal ban on social media for users under 16 is more convincing.

The proponents of the ban make a strong case based on evidence linking early social media exposure to increased mental health risks such as anxiety, depression, and low self-esteem. They emphasize the vulnerability of developing brains and liken the ban to existing age restrictions on harmful products like alcohol and tobacco, arguing that protecting mental health requires similar safeguards. This appeal to public health and neurological development is compelling and highlights a genuine concern for youth wellbeing.

However, the opposition presents several critical points that undercut the practicality and efficacy of a legal ban. First, social media is integral to young people's social lives, education, and self-expression; a ban risks isolating them socially, which could itself harm mental health. This recognizes the complexity of adolescent social needs and the reality that digital connectivity is now a core part of development. Second, enforcing such a ban is likely to be ineffective given the ease of circumventing age restrictions, potentially pushing underage users toward unregulated and riskier platforms, paradoxically increasing harm rather than reducing it. Third, the argument that mental health issues tied to social media use are complex and depend more on platform design, user education, and parental involvement is persuasive. It suggests that nuanced, multi-faceted strategies focusing on digital literacy and safer online environments may achieve better outcomes than blunt legal prohibitions.

In sum, while the goal of protecting youth mental health is laudable and urgently needed, a legal ban under 16 oversimplifies the issue and may create unintended negative consequences. The opposition’s argument acknowledges the social realities and rights of young people, focusing on practical, holistic solutions rather than prohibitive measures that are difficult to enforce and potentially counterproductive.

Therefore, the opposition’s case against legally banning social media access for under-16s is more compelling based on the presented arguments.
